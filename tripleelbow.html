the triple elbow

a new adaptive gaming system.

the problem.

Individuals with spinal cord injuries play video games as a form of therapy, using adaptive controllers that can be activated by their shoulders, head, and facial features. However, there is an issue in existing adaptive controllers: it is difficult to keep the controllers in place, resulting in constant disruption of gameplaying and significant effort from the therapist to keep the game running smoothly. The Shirley Ryan AbilityLab reached out to me and a few of my peers to design a solution to the problem of adaptive game controller instability.

the process.

After initially meeting with our client to discuss challenges in adaptive video gaming, we visited the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab to observe these challenges ourselves. We performed background research as well in order to fully comprehend the experiences of individuals with spinal cord injuries, as well as the various forms of therapy offered to them. We also considered existing adaptive video gaming devices, and analyzed the benefits and drawbacks of each design.

We spent several weeks brainstorming, designing, discussing the pros and cons of each design. We met with our peers and professors for a review of our primitive designs and consequently began to refine our top design picks.

the solution.

Ultimately our final design, the Triple Elbow, consisted of a base for the therapist to place behind a wheelchair which wraps around its sides, and a channeled aluminum post extending vertically from the base. On this post are rotationally adjustable arms that the therapist can loosen to adjust, then tighten to stabilize when the buttons attached to the end reach their ideal placement. The buttons are held by a clamping case which the user can loosen to insert a button, and then tighten to hold it in place. As a stand-alone device that can hold up to four adaptive gaming controls, aided by four extensive articulating arms, the Triple Elbow allows for quick adaptability between patients and gaming sessions.

the outcome.

Our design was presented at Northwestern University’s Design and Communication project fair, and then taken for use at the Shirley Ryan AbilityLab.
